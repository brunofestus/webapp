01 Do grazed areas accumulate more carbon than ungrazed areas?
Mean SOC Stock by Area Type (t C/ha)
SOC stocks suggest that ungrazed areas marginally exceed grazed areas within the protected area. However, this might be because most of the ungrazed areas were disproportionately located at higher altitudes where SOC is naturally high. Elevation therefore acted as a confounding factor. To isolate the true effect of wildebeest migratory grazing, SOC was normalised against elevation using OLS regression and the residual values showed SOC variation likely explained by grazing.
02 Residual SOC
Mean Residual SOC — Grazed vs Ungrazed
What is residual SOC in this context? It is the difference between an area's observed SOC and the SOC value predicted by terrain alone. A positive residual means in that location more carbon is stored than what can be explained by terrain alone pointing to an additional factor other than terrain.
After controlling for elevation, grazed areas show a mean residual SOC of +2.16 t C/ha while ungrazed areas show −3.14 t C/ha — consistent with a positive influence of wildebeest migratory grazing on soil carbon accrual.

Summary of Statistical Test Results
All tests confirmed that grazed areas have significantly higher residual SOC than ungrazed areas, with effect sizes indicating a meaningful and consistent grazing effect beyond terrain effects.

02 Multivariate Regression — Drivers of SOC Variation
Drivers of SOC — Standardised Effect Sizes
A multivariate regression model (Adj. R² = 0.452, F = 520.4, p < 2.2×10⁻¹⁶) explained ~45% of SOC variation across the Mara–Serengeti using three predictors. Elevation dominated as the strongest driver, grazing showed a meaningful positive effect, and rainfall had only a weak negative effect. All VIF values < 5, confirming no multicollinearity.
